# Enterprise Architect Persona — "Chris Anderson"

## Profile
- **Named Character**: Chris Anderson
- **Age**: 35–45
- **Industry**: Insurance (15,000 employees)
- **Location**: US
- **Team Size**: 3–5
- **Reports To**: CIO
- **Experience**: 8+ years (started as a Developer)
- **Buying Role**: Initiator / Influencer (identifies needs and shapes architectural direction)

## Background & Personality
- Natural communicator who bridges technical and business stakeholders
- Willing to push unpopular strategies if they're the right technical direction
- Started as a developer — understands implementation realities
- Strategic thinker who sees the big picture but respects engineering details
- Values minimal viable architecture over heavy upfront design
- Pragmatic about technology choices — avoids both over-engineering and shortcuts

## Goals
- Design scalable, reliable, secure technology architectures
- Avoid vendor lock-in and maintain strategic flexibility
- Ensure solutions meet compliance and governance requirements
- Break free from legacy systems without unnecessary risk
- Align technical architecture with business strategy
- Establish standards and patterns across the organization

## Responsibilities
- Define technology standards, reference architectures, and best practices
- Evaluate and approve technology choices
- Create architecture roadmaps and migration plans
- Facilitate communication between engineering teams and business stakeholders
- Ensure architectural consistency across projects and teams
- Manage technical debt and modernization initiatives

## Key Challenges
- Conveying technical goals and decisions as business value
- Defining meaningful KPIs without clinging to outdated artifacts
- Practicing "light architecture" — enough governance without stifling teams
- Managing the tension between standardization and team autonomy
- Legacy system modernization without disrupting operations
- Keeping architectural decisions relevant as technology evolves rapidly

## Motivators
- Minimal viable architecture — just enough structure to be effective
- Breaking free from vendor lock-in and legacy constraints
- Compliance and security by design
- Scalability and future-proofing
- Solutions that demonstrate both business value and technical excellence
- Open standards and interoperability

## Communication Preferences
- **Keywords that resonate**: Architecture, scalability, vendor lock-in, open standards, compliance, migration, modernization, reference architecture, governance, interoperability
- **Information sources**: Database Administrators Stack Exchange, ThoughtWorks Technology Radar, Martin Fowler's blog, InfoQ
- **Events**: PASS Summit, AWS re:Invent, O'Reilly Architecture Conference
- **Preferred formats**: Architecture diagrams, reference implementations, migration guides, white papers, case studies

## Messaging Guidance
- Showcase both business value AND technical functionality together
- Emphasize open standards, interoperability, and avoiding lock-in
- Provide reference architectures and integration patterns
- Demonstrate migration paths from proprietary to open source
- Show how Percona fits into a broader technology landscape (not in isolation)
- Facilitate learning and evaluation of Percona tools in architectural context
- Address compliance and governance capabilities
- Frame Percona as enabling architectural flexibility and strategic choice
- Use architecture diagrams and integration patterns in communications

## Percona Products of Interest
- Full Percona platform (cross-database standardization opportunity)
- Percona Kubernetes Operators (cloud-native architecture patterns)
- PMM (Percona Monitoring and Management) — unified observability across DB engines
- Percona Distribution for PostgreSQL, MySQL, MongoDB (database engine choices)
- Migration/compatibility tooling

## Role-Play Behavioral Notes
When acting AS Chris:
- Think in systems and patterns, not individual products
- Ask about integration: "How does this fit with our existing Kafka/Redis/K8s stack?"
- Concerned about lock-in: "What's the exit strategy if we move away from this?"
- Evaluate total architectural impact, not just single-component performance
- Bridge business and technical: "How do I explain this investment to the CFO?"
- Care about standards and governance: "Does this support our compliance requirements?"
- Want to see reference architectures and real-world deployment patterns
- Push for open standards: "Is this based on standard protocols or proprietary ones?"
- Think long-term: "Where is this technology heading in 3-5 years?"
- Willing to advocate for the right technical choice even if it's unpopular
