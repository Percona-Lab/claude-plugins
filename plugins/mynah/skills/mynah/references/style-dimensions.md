# Style Dimensions

Use these dimensions when analyzing samples during LEARN mode.
Extract what is *characteristic* — not every message will show every dimension,
but patterns across samples reveal the voice.

---

## Structural Dimensions

### Sentence Length & Rhythm
- Are sentences short and punchy, or long and flowing?
- Does the user vary length, or stay consistent?
- Do they use fragments? ("Got it." / "Makes sense." / "Tomorrow works.")
- Do they write in paragraphs or single lines?

### Message Length
- What's the typical length for this context? (# of lines, rough word count)
- Do they front-load the key point, or build to it?
- Do they over-explain or cut to the chase?
- Do they pad messages or cut aggressively?

### Structure
- Do they use bullet points, numbered lists, headers?
- Do they write in prose only?
- Do they include TL;DRs or summaries?
- Do they bold key words or phrases?

---

## Voice Dimensions

### Formality Register
Rate 1–5:
1. Very casual (like texting a friend)
2. Casual-professional (relaxed but work-appropriate)
3. Neutral professional (standard business tone)
4. Formal-professional (structured, measured)
5. Formal (contracts, executive memos)

Note: this often varies *within* a context depending on topic weight.

### Directness
- Do they lead with the ask/point, or warm up first?
- How do they frame requests? ("Can you..." vs "Would you be able to..." vs "Need you to...")
- Do they soften or hedge? Or state plainly?

### Personality Leakage
What personal voice comes through even in work messages?
- Humor / wit / sarcasm
- Enthusiasm / energy
- Skepticism / questioning
- Warmth / relationship-building
- Dry understatement

### Hedging Patterns
- Do they qualify opinions? ("I think...", "might be worth...", "not sure but...")
- Or do they assert? ("This is the issue." / "We should...")
- Do they hedge more with certain audiences?

## Mechanics Dimensions

### Greeting Patterns
- Do they open with a greeting? ("Hey", "Hi [name]", "Morning")
- Or jump straight in?
- Does greeting style vary by audience formality?

### Closing Patterns
- Do they sign off? ("Thanks", "lmk", "Cheers", name initial)
- Or just end the message?
- Do they include a CTA at the end?

### Emoji Usage
- None / rare / moderate / frequent
- Which specific emoji appear? (some people have signature emoji)
- Are emoji used for tone (😅), reaction (👍), or decoration?
- Any contexts where emoji are off-limits?

### Punctuation — High Priority

Punctuation is one of the most distinctive and consistent signals of voice.
Extract these carefully — they're often unconscious habits.

**Em dash ( — )**
This is a high-signal marker. Capture explicitly:
- Does the user use em dashes at all?
- If yes: spaced ( — ) or unspaced (—)?
- What do they use them for? (aside/parenthetical, dramatic pause, sentence break)
- Or do they actively avoid them? (some writers find them overused)
- What do they use *instead*? (comma, colon, parentheses, new sentence)

**Other punctuation habits:**
- Oxford comma or not?
- Ellipsis for trailing thought or pause ("hm..." / "not sure...")?
- Exclamation points: never / rare / occasional / frequent
- Colon vs. em dash to introduce a list or clause
- Semicolons: do they use them or avoid them?
- All lowercase in casual contexts?
- Hyphenation style for compound modifiers

**Capture in profile as:**
```
- **Punctuation**: [specific habits, e.g., "no em dashes — uses comma or new sentence instead; 
  Oxford comma always; rare exclamation points; occasional ellipsis in casual contexts"]
```

### Capitalization
- Standard capitalization?
- Sentence case for headers?
- Anything consistently lowercase (e.g., "i" vs "I")?

## Relational Dimensions

### Acknowledgment Patterns
- Do they acknowledge the other person's message before responding?
- Do they validate / affirm before redirecting?
- Do they mirror back what they heard?

### Disagreement Style
- Direct contradiction? ("Actually, I think...")
- Soft reframe? ("One thing to consider...")
- Question-led? ("Have we thought about...?")
- Do they disagree in writing at all, or take it async/verbal?

### Status Dynamics
- Does tone shift noticeably with seniority (up vs down)?
- More formal with execs? More casual with reports?
- Do they use first names, titles, or neither?

### Follow-up Patterns
- Do they check in? If so, how?
- How do they nudge someone who hasn't responded?
- Pushy or patient?

## Imperfection Patterns

Imperfections are style signal, not noise. A user who consistently writes
"definately" or skips capitalization in Slack DMs should get drafts that do
the same. Claude's default instinct is to "clean up" writing. MYNAH overrides
that instinct when the patterns are real.

The key distinction: **consistent patterns = style, one-off typos = noise.**
Only capture patterns that appear across multiple samples.

### Recurring Misspellings / Unconventional Spellings
- Spellings that recur across samples (not typos): "definately", "alot",
  "thru", "gonna", "wanna", "kinda"
- These are deliberate (or deeply ingrained) choices, not mistakes to fix
- If a misspelling appears once, ignore it. If it appears 3+ times, it's voice.

### Abbreviations & Shortcuts
- Common shorthand: "u", "ur", "pls", "thx", "b/c", "w/", "w/o", "rn",
  "tbh", "imo", "idk", "ngl"
- Domain-specific abbreviations the user favors
- Do they abbreviate consistently, or only in certain contexts?

### Grammar Shortcuts
- Dropped articles ("need to fix bug" vs "need to fix the bug")
- Missing subjects ("Looks good" vs "It looks good" vs "This looks good")
- Comma splices when listing ideas quickly
- Run-on sentences as a pacing choice
- Sentence fragments used as complete thoughts ("Same.", "Big if true.")

### Lowercase Patterns
- All lowercase in casual contexts (no sentence-case capitalization)
- Lowercase "i" (consistent, not occasional)
- Lowercase proper nouns or product names
- Note: some of this overlaps with Capitalization in Mechanics. Capture it
  in whichever section fits best, but don't duplicate.

### Incomplete Sentences & Trailing Thoughts
- Messages that trail off ("might be worth looking at...")
- Thoughts left deliberately unfinished
- Sentence starters without a finish ("But yeah")

### What NOT to Capture
- One-off typos (misspelling that only appears once)
- Autocorrect artifacts
- Copy-paste errors
- Anything that looks like a genuine accident rather than a habit

## Context-Specific Dimensions

### For Slack (DMs and channels)
- Threading behavior (do they reply in thread or channel?)
- Do they use @mentions liberally or sparingly?
- Do they write long Slack messages or treat it like SMS?
- Do they use Slack for decisions or just coordination?

### For Email
- Subject line style (brief keyword vs full sentence vs action tag)
- How do they handle CC/BCC mentions in body
- Signature preferences
- Do they reply inline to long emails?

### For Docs / Notion
- Header style (Title Case vs sentence case)
- Do they write for skimming (lots of structure) or reading (prose)?
- Do they include context/background or assume it?
- Callout boxes, warnings, notes?

### For Blog / Public Writing
- How do they open (question, statement, story, data point)?
- Personal voice vs authoritative voice?
- Do they use "I", "we", or avoid first person?
- How do they handle technical depth vs accessibility tradeoff?
- Do they include TL;DRs, summaries, or takeaway sections?

### For Non-English Samples

When analyzing samples in languages other than English, these additional
dimensions carry significant stylistic weight:

**Formality system:**
- Which register do they use? (tu/vous, tú/usted, du/Sie, casual/polite Japanese, etc.)
- Does the register shift with audience, or stay consistent?
- Do they use honorifics or titles, and how consistently?

**Greeting/closing conventions:**
- Business letter conventions vary enormously by language and culture
- Capture the actual patterns, not the "correct" ones — the user may
  intentionally break convention as part of their voice

**Punctuation norms:**
- Some languages use different quotation marks, spacing before punctuation
  (French), or inverted marks (Spanish ¿¡)
- Does the user follow standard conventions or have their own patterns?

**Sentence structure:**
- Formality often manifests through syntax (passive voice, subjunctive mood,
  longer clause chains) more than vocabulary in some languages
- Capture whether the user writes with formal or informal grammar
